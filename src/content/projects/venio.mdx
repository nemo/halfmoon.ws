Venio was a Toronto-based mobile app I co-founded that tackled a deceptively simple, everyday problem: figuring out what to eat. We launched on iOS in November 2012, alongside a companion recipe-discovery web app and an internal tool we built for the recipe contributors who fed our content pipeline.

The premise was that meal decisions are personal and repetitive, so they should be both tailored and effortless. Users set their dietary preferences and restrictions — vegan, vegetarian, with gluten-free and allergy filters on the roadmap — and Venio returned personalized meal suggestions matched to different times of day. Rather than maintain a static recipe database, we sourced meals from noted food bloggers and quality content so the recommendations stayed fresh, and we tracked the meals users actually marked as eaten to keep learning what worked for them.

That focus on habit paid off quickly. Within two weeks of launch we had over 5,000 users, roughly 3,000 meals recorded, and around 30% daily active users — stickiness that was unusually high for the category. The plan from there was to deepen the experience through partnerships with diet brands like Atkins and the Sonoma Diet, and to explore collaborations with restaurants and grocers.

Venio ultimately wound down — there was no funding round or acquisition — but the experience sharpened how I think about retention and habit-forming product design, lessons I've carried into everything I've built since.
