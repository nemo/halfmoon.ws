Sound Hallucinations is an experimental audiovisual performance I created and performed in. Each set pairs a musician with a visual artist, and the two play as a single instrument: projection-mapped imagery wraps every wall of a darkened room while live electronic and acoustic sound moves through it. Sometimes the visuals lead and the sound follows; sometimes the sound drives the picture. Either way there is an audio-reactive relationship between the two, so that what you hear and what you see are never quite separable.

The idea was to treat the visual artists as players rather than backdrop. They built their own instruments — their own knobs, their own controllers, one of them quite literally running lasers out of his laptop — so they could shape what the audience sees as fluidly as a musician shapes a phrase. The night unfolds as a series of vignettes, short pieces by different pairs, each a small experiment in this form.

## The room

We performed in a space we could fully take over — walls we could project across and a sound system worth the effort. That is harder to find than it sounds, and it took weeks of borrowing a living room to rehearse and a generous venue to host. Over the course of an evening the room itself became the medium: old-growth redwood forest stretched corner to corner, soft indigo cloudscapes drifted across the plaster, and a glittering starfield enveloped the audience while they sat on rugs and cushions on the floor.

The performers worked from a central island of gear in the middle of the room — stacked synthesizers and keyboards, a mixer, modular and effects boxes, microphones, and a tangle of patch cables, all lit by a single work lamp. The audience surrounded them on all sides, closer to a shared ritual than a stage show.

## The performance

I opened the evening with Edson Pavoni on a piece titled *Dawn*, the two of us at the central rig as a particle-driven visual bloomed across the screen behind us. From there the night moved through additional pairings of musicians and visual artists, each set its own self-contained world built from the same vocabulary of projected light and live sound.

Before the music began I asked the audience to close their eyes for a few moments — a short guided sequence to heighten the senses and let everyone settle into the room before the first hallucination took hold. The whole thing depended on a small crew: the people who lent their living room for weeks of rehearsal, the designer who shaped the space, the person documenting it, and the venue that made a fully projectable, properly amplified room possible.
