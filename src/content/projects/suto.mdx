I cofounded Suto, a conversational commerce bot that helped people make confident, well-researched purchasing decisions. The idea was born out of my own frustration: I once spent two weeks researching a projector and still couldn't comfortably decide what to buy. Suto — reachable at AskSuto.com and through SMS, MMS, email, and Facebook Messenger — was the personified concierge I wished I'd had during that ordeal.

## How it worked

People asked Suto the kinds of questions you'd normally agonize over for days: "What's the best graphics card under $300?" or "A good pair of headphones for working out?" A small team — six of us, including the two cofounders — would research each request, pooling Amazon listings and published reviews, and come back with personalized recommendations and purchase links. Most answers landed within six to twelve hours, often in under four.

Early on we wired up natural-language processing and Twilio to handle intake and routing, but the actual research was done by humans. Suto felt like a single, trustworthy personality on the other end of the conversation, even though there was a whole team behind it.

## Traction

We launched officially in November 2015 after some informal testing that summer, and went through a Y Combinator Fellowship. Within about four to six weeks of testing, roughly one in three users were actually buying the products we recommended. The questions spanned headphones, TVs, mattresses, strollers, laptops, baby products, and more, and we partnered with around a dozen retailers, including Amazon.

## Why it ended

Our revenue model leaned on affiliate commissions, primarily through Amazon, alongside some attempted direct sales. The cracks showed over time: the NLP was too error-prone and leaned heavily on human intervention, and even when people loved our recommendations, they often trusted established retailers over the bot to actually complete the purchase. Only about a third of the purchases users told us about ever translated into affiliate revenue, and that economics couldn't sustain the operation.

So we made the hard call to wind Suto down and channel everything we'd learned about conversational interfaces, recommendation, and trust into building a faster revenue-generating business — which became Pearmill.
